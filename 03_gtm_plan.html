§ 01 · The why

What we're actually selling.

We are not selling AI. We are not selling agents. We are not selling video. The customer is buying:

  • Throughput. A 6-person Hyderabad ad shop ships ~40 ad creatives a month. With us they ship 200. Same team, no new hires.
  • Language coverage they can't afford to staff. Telugu, Tamil, Marathi, Bangla — they can't keep voice talent on retainer for each. We can.
  • Character consistency they can't get from Runway alone. Their D2C clients reject inconsistent mascots. We solve it.
  • Predictable cost per asset. ₹250 each, billed Friday. Not three vendors and four invoices.

The "AI agent factory" is the how, not the what. The customer doesn't care whether six agents or one model produces the asset. They care whether the asset reads as on-brand and ships on time.

The product is throughput. The agents are plumbing. The pitch is "your D2C client wants 30 variants in 5 languages by Friday — say yes."
§ 02 · Disruption — honest

The disruption story — but not the one in the brief.

The brief asked: how do we disrupt the traditional agency model? I'm rejecting that framing because agencies are our customers, not our targets. Here's the disruption that's actually true:

LayerOld worldWhat we change
The cost of an ad creative ₹3,000–₹15,000 per asset (freelancer + editor + voice) ₹250–₹400. The vendor stack collapses into one invoice.
The unit of work "A campaign" — billed per project, slow, scopey "An asset" — billed per credit, fast, atomic
The constraint on language coverage Voice talent + dub studio per language API call, ₹3 per minute, 8 languages on day one
The agency's economics Margin from staff utilization Margin from tool leverage. The 6-person shop competes with the 25-person shop.

Who actually gets disrupted: not "agencies." It's the staff inside agencies who do work that's now done by tools — specifically, junior video editors, voice-over freelancers in regional languages, and storyboard artists. The agency itself, if it adopts us, gets stronger. The agency that doesn't adopt us is the one that gets disrupted — but it's disrupted by us only via the agency next door.

The thing not to say in any pitch Never tell an ad-shop founder you're disrupting them. Tell them you're the reason they outpriced the larger agency in the next building. Same product, different positioning. Pick the one that closes.
§ 03 · North Star

The one number that decides if this is working.

North Star — AAPCW
Approved
Assets / Customer / Week
An asset the customer downloaded AND used. Not generated. Not delivered. Used.

Why this metric, not the obvious ones

Tempting NSMWhy I'm rejecting it
MRR / ARR Lagging. Trails reality by 60–90 days. A bad product with good sales hits ARR targets and dies the next quarter.
Assets generated Vanity. We can generate millions and have no business if customers don't ship them.
Active customers Doesn't separate the customer who pays ₹4,999 and uses 2 assets from the one who pays ₹39,999 and uses 180.
Critique pass rate Our judgment of our own work. The customer's judgment is what matters.
NPS What people say. AAPCW is what they do.

Why AAPCW works. If we're producing assets the customer actually ships, three things must be true: the quality is good enough, the cost is justifiable, and the workflow fits how they work. If any of those breaks, AAPCW drops first — before MRR, before NPS, before churn shows up. It's the earliest possible signal of product–market fit and the earliest possible signal of failure.

§ 04 · Blue Ocean

Three Blue Ocean niches.

"Blue Ocean" means a market segment where the competitive set isn't another AI video tool — it's the absence of any tool, or the absence of a tool that speaks the customer's language. I've ranked these in order of fit, not size.

Niche 01 · Lead

Regional-Language D2C Performance Ad Shops

Hyderabad & Bengaluru ad shops serving Telugu/Tamil-speaking D2C brands
ICP size~400 shops
Avg ticket₹15k–₹40k/mo
Sales motionFounder-led
CAC~₹3k
Why blue: US tools (HeyGen, Synthesia, Captions) are weak on Telugu/Tamil dub. Pixis and Rocketium go enterprise. Local agencies have no AI-native tool built for their workflow. We're the only sharp option.
Niche 02 · Adjacent

Indian Coaching Creators (Finance, Career, Exam Prep)

TagMango / Graphy / Rigi creators producing daily explainer reels
ICP size~50k creators
Avg ticket₹3k–₹12k/mo
Sales motionSelf-serve
CAC~₹500
Why blue: They produce 2–5 explainer reels/day. They don't have an editor. They don't have voice variety. They want avatar-led shorts in their language, fast. Currently bridged with InVideo + ChatGPT — clunky.
Niche 03 · Future

Tier-2 City Real Estate & Auto Dealers

Hyderabad / Pune / Indore property & vehicle dealers running WhatsApp + Reels-led inside sales
ICP size~10k dealerships
Avg ticket₹8k–₹25k/mo
Sales motionChannel partner
CAC~₹2k
Why blue: They want walkthrough videos, listing reels, regional-language voice-over. Currently done by random freelancers. No incumbent. But: sales cycle is slow, project-based, hard to retain. Park for Year 2.

Why these three, in this order

  • Niche 1 is highest leverage now. Each ad-shop customer brings 5–20 of their D2C clients into our orbit. It's a B2B2B motion that compounds.
  • Niche 2 is the biggest by count but lowest ticket. Self-serve only works after the product is rock-solid (M6+). Dragged forward, it would burn support hours.
  • Niche 3 is real money but slow. Channel-partner sales (working through real-estate marketing consultancies) is a separate sales motion entirely. Year 2.
§ 05 · Wedge

Pick one. Niche 1.

For the first 12 months, the only customer is: creative shops and digital ad agencies of 5–25 people, in Hyderabad and Bengaluru, who serve at least three D2C brands needing regional-language ad creative.

That sentence is the company's spine for one year. Anyone who doesn't fit it gets a polite "we're not the right fit yet." This is not arrogance — it's discipline. Every "yes" outside this ICP eats founder time that the ICP needs.

What this means in practice If a wedding videographer emails asking to use the platform — say no. If a SaaS founder wants product demo videos — no. If a school wants e-learning content — no. Every "no" outside Niche 1 is a "yes" to Niche 1. Founder focus is the scarcest resource. Spend it on one room.

The weekly sales cadence (Q2 onwards)

  1. Mon morning (commute): Review last week's funnel. 3 questions: who didn't reply, who needs follow-up, who closed.
  2. Tue/Thu lunch (45 min each): 5 LinkedIn DMs each. Specific. Personalized to one D2C client they recently posted about.
  3. Wed evening (1 hr): One discovery call. Default 6 PM IST.
  4. Fri evening (1 hr): One demo OR one customer success call (alternating).
  5. Sat morning (90 min): Pipeline review + Loom case-study video update for existing customers.
  6. Sun: Off. Non-negotiable. Daughter, wife, F&O book.
Why this cadence works at 10 hrs/week Sales is rhythm, not blocks. 30 minutes daily beats 4 hours on Sunday. The cadence above produces ~16 conversations/week at peak, which is the funnel-throughput math required to hit 30 customers by M12.

§ 11 · Kill criteria

When I walk away.

Pre-committed because future-me will be motivated to ignore the data.

  1. End of Q1 (M3): If <3 active design partners using the system weekly — kill.
  2. End of Q2 (M6): If <5 paying customers AND AAPCW <3 — kill.
  3. End of Q3 (M9): If MRR <₹2L OR gross margin <25% — pause & re-architect; both fail → kill.
  4. End of Q4 (M12): If MRR <₹6L OR I'm working >15 hrs/week — kill or sell.
  5. Anytime: A foundation-model vendor ships an India-localised creative agent at <₹0.5/asset → exit gracefully, sell what's left, move on.
  6. Anytime: Day-job performance review drops a tier — kill. The salary is the foundation.
  7. Anytime: Family signals real strain (wife flags, daughter notices) — kill. Non-negotiable.
Promise I will treat these as data, not failure. The point of this venture is optionality, not validation of my ego. If I read this in 11 months and my instinct is to argue with one of these criteria, that's the moment I most need to honor it.
§ 12 · The look-back

Twelve months from now.

If this plan worked, on 26 April 2027 I will be able to say:

  • 30 paying customers, mostly in Hyderabad/Bengaluru ad-agency segment.
  • ₹6–10L MRR, gross margin ≥45%, healthy customer retention.
  • One part-time engineer hired in Q3, productive by Q4.
  • 3 case studies published, with named D2C client logos.
  • The product makes 8+ approved assets per customer per week — embedded in workflow.
  • I'm working 12 hrs/week, not 30. The vidaXL job is unaffected.
  • The decision in front of me is "raise ₹2–4 cr to expand languages and ICPs, or grow on revenue alone." Either is fine.

If those are true, the Year 2 plan writes itself: more languages, more ICPs (Niche 2, then Niche 3), and the option to start the creator channel as marketing flywheel. The vision in the triptych at the top of this document — Indian regional creators outshipping US studios — becomes a 4-year claim, not a 5-year one.

If those are not true, the kill criteria above tell me what to do instead. Either way, this document was useful — it forced specificity in April 2026 that future-me cannot pretend wasn't there.

The vision is large. The first 365 days are not. Both are true at the same time, and the second one is the only one I control.
